How I Price Copywriting Projects (and Why Hourly Loses)

By Kho Wata · 7 min read

Early on, I billed by the hour. It felt fair — you pay for the time you get. But hourly billing quietly punishes everyone involved, and once I switched to fixed, value-based pricing, both my clients and I were better off. Here's the reasoning, and the process.

Why hourly billing works against you

Hourly pricing ties your income to time, which creates three problems:

  • It penalises efficiency. The faster and better you get, the less you earn for the same result. That's backwards.
  • It makes clients anxious. An open-ended clock turns every revision into a cost worry, so people ask for less than they need.
  • It prices the wrong thing. A client doesn't want hours of writing. They want a page that converts. Charge for the result, not the raw material.

Value-based pricing, in plain terms

Fixed, value-based pricing means quoting one number for a clearly defined outcome, based on what that outcome is worth to the client — not how long it takes me. A sales page that lifts conversions is worth far more than the hours behind it, and pricing to the value lets me invest the time it truly deserves.

Clients don't buy your time. They buy the difference your work makes.

The process I use to quote

  1. Understand the goal. What is this copy meant to achieve, and what's it worth if it does?
  2. Define the scope. Exactly what's included — pages, revisions, timeline — so there are no surprises.
  3. Quote one fixed price. A single number for the whole project, sent within a day.
  4. Cap revisions clearly. Two rounds included keeps quality high without an endless clock.

Why clients prefer it too

A fixed price removes fear. The client knows the total before they commit, can budget with certainty, and never has to weigh "is this revision worth the money?" against getting the work right. That freedom produces better collaboration — and better copy.

The takeaway

Whether you're hiring a freelancer or working as one, price the outcome, not the hours. It aligns everyone around the same goal: work that's genuinely worth what it costs.
