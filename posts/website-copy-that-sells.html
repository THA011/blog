How to Write Website Copy That Actually Sells

By Kho Wata · 5 min read

A visitor lands on your homepage and, within about five seconds, decides whether to stay. In those seconds they're asking three questions. If your copy answers them fast, you've earned the scroll. If it doesn't, no amount of design will save the page.

The three questions every homepage must answer

  1. What is this? — In plain words, what do you offer?
  2. Is it for me? — Does the reader recognise themselves and their problem?
  3. What do I do next? — What's the obvious, low-friction action?

Your hero section — the first thing above the fold — exists to answer questions one and two. Your call to action answers the third. Everything else on the page is support.

Write the headline for the reader, not the company

Weak headlines describe the business ("Welcome to Acme Solutions"). Strong headlines describe the reader's desired outcome ("Get paid faster, without chasing invoices"). The reader doesn't care who you are yet — they care what you can do for them.

Structure the page as a conversation

Good page copy anticipates the reader's next thought and answers it in order:

  • Hook: the outcome they want.
  • Problem: the friction standing in the way.
  • Solution: how you remove it.
  • Proof: evidence it works.
  • Ask: the single next step.

Each section should make the reader nod and keep scrolling. If a section doesn't move them closer to "yes," it's decoration.

One page, one action

Decide the single most valuable thing a visitor can do — book a call, start a trial, send an enquiry — and design the whole page to lead there. Repeat that one call to action; don't dilute it with five competing links.

Design gets people to read your copy. Copy gets people to act. You need both, but the words carry the decision.

Rewrite your homepage against these rules and read it out loud. If a stranger couldn't tell what you do, who it's for, and what to do next, keep cutting until they can.
