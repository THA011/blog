5 Copywriting Principles That Turn Readers Into Clients

By Kho Wata · 6 min read

Most business copy describes. Great copy persuades. The gap between them isn't talent or clever wording — it's a handful of principles applied consistently. Here are the five I return to on every project.

1. Write for one person, not an audience

"Audience" is an abstraction, and abstractions produce bland copy. Picture a single reader: their situation, the problem keeping them up at night, the words they'd use to describe it. Then write to that one person. Counter-intuitively, the more specific you are, the more people feel you're talking to them.

2. Lead with the benefit, support with the feature

Features are what your product is. Benefits are what your reader gets. People buy the second and rationalise with the first.

"Automatic backups every hour" is a feature. "You'll never lose a day's work again" is why anyone cares.

Name the outcome first, then let the feature prove it's real.

3. Be clear before you try to be clever

A pun the reader has to decode is a toll booth on the road to "yes." Clever copy that's also clear is a bonus; clever copy that costs comprehension is vanity. When in doubt, cut the wordplay and say the true thing plainly.

4. Earn belief with proof

Every claim raises a silent objection: says who? Answer it before it hardens into doubt. Specifics, testimonials, numbers, guarantees and named results all convert scepticism into trust. Vague superlatives ("the best," "world-class") do the opposite — they signal you have nothing concrete to offer.

5. Ask for one thing

A page that asks for five actions gets none of them. Decide the single most valuable step a reader can take, then make that ask obvious and remove every competing distraction around it. One page, one goal.

Putting it together

Take any page of your copy and run it through these five checks: one reader, benefit-led, clear, proven, one ask. Most copy fails at least three. Fixing them is often the cheapest growth you'll ever buy.
