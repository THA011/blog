A beautiful website that doesn't convert is expensive decoration. Design isn't
there to impress other designers — it's there to remove every reason a reader
might hesitate. Here's how good design quietly does sales work.

## Hierarchy tells the eye where to go

Readers don't read pages; they scan them. Visual hierarchy — size, weight,
colour and spacing — decides what they notice first. The most important thing on
the page (usually your headline and call to action) should be the most visually
dominant. If everything shouts, nothing is heard.

## Speed is a conversion feature

Every extra second of load time costs you visitors before they've read a word.
Lightweight pages, optimised images and minimal scripts aren't just technical
hygiene — they're the difference between a reader who stays and one who's already
gone.

## Whitespace is not wasted space

Crowded layouts feel stressful and cheap. Generous spacing gives each element
room to be understood, and signals confidence — you don't need to cram because
you know what matters. Restraint reads as quality.

## Design mobile-first

Most visitors arrive on a phone. If your layout only works on a wide screen,
you've designed for the minority. Start with the smallest screen, make the single
action easy to tap, and let the layout expand from there.

> Good design makes the right choice feel effortless. Great design makes the
> wrong choice feel unnecessary.

## Design and copy are one job

The most common mistake is treating words and layout as separate stages. They're
not. When I design a page, I'm designing how the message unfolds — where the eye
rests, when the proof appears, how the call to action stays within reach. Copy
and design succeed or fail together.
